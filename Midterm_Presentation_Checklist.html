Presentation Checklist

Tap each section to open it. Tick off each point as you prepare.

How to use this: Work through each step when preparing. On presentation day, use it as a running order — each section is one "slide" or moment in your talk. You do not need to show every slide. You need to tell a story.
1
Hook
⌄
  • ✓
    Show a photo or video of your finished artwork
  • ✓
    Say the title — clearly, with confidence
  • ✓
    Say nothing else yet. Let them look.
Why: Starting with the image creates anticipation. They form their own first impression before you influence it — that makes the final reveal more powerful.
✓ Done
2
Inspiration
Wallas: preparation ⌄
Start with a question or a story — not a statement.
  • ✓
    What first sparked this idea? (A moment, an artwork, a feeling)
  • ✓
    Show your source of inspiration — an image, a song, a memory
  • ✓
    Show your early brainstorm or research notes if you have them
  • ✓
    Make us feel why this mattered to you
Try: "Have you ever felt..." or "I saw something that I could not stop thinking about..." — then show what it was.
✓ Done
3
Exploration
Wallas: incubation ⌄
  • ✓
    What ideas did you try first?
  • ✓
    What did you reject — and why?
  • ✓
    What kept changing as you worked?
  • ✓
    Show sketches, drafts, rejected versions if you have them
Bonus: Did anything change unexpectedly while you were making it? Happy accidents belong here — they show your creative process honestly.
✓ Done
4
Final plan — introduce the art
⌄
  • ✓
    What did you decide on, and why?
  • ✓
    Show or describe the artwork clearly
  • ✓
    Give the audience enough time to look at it
Remember: Do not reveal your message yet. Just introduce what they are looking at.
✓ Done
5
Technical facts
⌄
  • ✓
    What is it made from? (Materials, medium)
  • ✓
    What tools or techniques did you use?
  • ✓
    Describe the making process — step by step if needed
  • ✓
    Stick to objective facts here — no feelings yet
Think of it like: if a museum card described your work, what would it say?
✓ Done
6
Intended effect
⌄
  • ✓
    What emotions did you want the viewer to feel?
  • ✓
    How does the work try to create those feelings? (Colour, form, composition, texture...)
  • ✓
    What genre or style does it belong to?
  • ✓
    Do NOT reveal the message yet — keep the reveal for the end
The gap matters: "I wanted people to feel X" sets up the final question — did they?
✓ Done
7
Reflection
⌄
  • ✓
    When you finished — what did you feel?
  • ✓
    Did those feelings match what you intended?
  • ✓
    What would you do differently?
  • ✓
    What surprised you?
Be honest. Saying "it did not go how I planned" is not a failure — it is good critical thinking.
✓ Done
★
Bonus — audience response
optional ⌄
  • ✓
    Did you show your artwork to anyone before today?
  • ✓
    What did they say or feel? Was it what you expected?
  • ✓
    How does this show Hall's theory of encoding and decoding?
  • ✓
    Did different people from different backgrounds read it differently?
  • ✓
    What does that tell you about art and communication?
Hall's theory: You encode a meaning into the work. The audience decodes it — but their culture, experience and context shapes what they see. Their reading may not match yours. That gap is not a mistake — it is the conversation art creates.
✓ Done
8
Reveal
⌄
  • ✓
    Show the photo or video of your artwork again
  • ✓
    Now reveal the message — what is this really about?
  • ✓
    Keep it short. One or two sentences. Let the art speak.
  • ✓
    Ask if anyone has questions
Biggest impact last. Everything in your presentation builds to this moment — do not rush it.
✓ Done

Quick order — on the day

1. Show artwork + title
5. Technical facts
2. Inspiration story
6. Intended emotions
3. What you explored
7. Your reflection
4. Final decision
8. Reveal the message
